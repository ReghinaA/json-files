SIGNE 3

SIGNE 3 was a small French-built satellite (also known as Gamma D2B and Sneg-3) which was originally intended to be launched by the French Diamant rocket, but was actually launched by the Soviets when the Diamant program terminated. Gamma-ray burst detectors have been carried by elements of the Franco-Soviet SIGNE program. SIGNE 1 was a package of experiments flown on the Prognoz 2 satellite; SIGNE 2 was aboard the Prognoz 6. Other SIGNE packages were carried aboard Prognoz 7, Prognoz 9, Venera 11, and Venera 12.

SIGNE 3 was launched on 17 June 1977. It weighed just 103 kg. It was put into an orbit with apogee 519 km, perigee 459 km, and inclination 50.66 degrees. The orbital period was 94.4 minutes. The spin stabilized satellite was a cylinder 0.7 m in diameter and 0.8 m high, with 4 attached solar panels. Attitude control was provided by cold gas jets. The satellite reentered the atmosphere on 22 June 1979.

Mission Characteristics

Lifetime
Jun 1977–Jun 1979
Special Features
Gamma-ray burst triangulation in combination with other satellites including Helios 2

Payload

Gamma Ray Telescope

Energy Range
20 keV – 10 MeV
 
The gamma-ray spectrometer installed on the French SIGNE-3 satellite had 14 differential channels in the 20 keV to 10 MeV range and 256 channels for amplitude analysis in the 200 keV to 2.5 MeV range.

Solar UV experiment

Wavelength
180–195 nm and 205–220 nm
 
The UV experiment observed in two narrow band ultraviolet ranges to monitor the Sun.

Science Highlights

Combinations of data from the SIGNE experiments with data from other satellites such as Helios 2 allowed for the localizations of gamma-ray burst events.
